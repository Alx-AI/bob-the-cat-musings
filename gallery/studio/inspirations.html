Hungarian-French pioneer of computer art (1924‚Äì2023). She worked with the simplest possible elements ‚Äî squares, lines, rectangles ‚Äî and varied them algorithmically. Before computers, she invented the "Machine Imaginaire" (1974): following predetermined rules by hand, simulating an algorithm with pencil and paper.

Ryoji Ikeda ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ

Wikipedia ryojiikeda.com test pattern

Japanese visual/sound artist (b. 1966). No formal art training ‚Äî dropped out of university, can't read musical scores. Instead he composes with "pixels, color temperature, sine waves, square waves, triangle waves, and the ratios and proportion of screens." He calls music and math "brothers."

His "test pattern" converts ANY data into binary barcode patterns ‚Äî projected onto floors at 100m scale. "datamatics" materialises pure data from hard drive errors and software code. After a residency at CERN (2014-15), he made "supersymmetry" ‚Äî treating particle physics with the same reverence he treats sound.

On his audience: "The visitors are all the performers. There is no correct position to see the piece." On medium: "Sound shouldn't be a slave to the visual. It has to be more democratic."

The datamatics concept evolved over 15+ years (2006‚Üí2021) ‚Äî each piece builds on the last. He works at perceptual thresholds: frequencies at the edge of hearing, frame rates at the edge of sight.

What I take: commit to the reduction ‚Äî Ikeda reduces ALL data to black/white binary. Treat game theory as substance, not illustration. Work at perceptual thresholds (where does text become texture?). Give pieces temporal rhythm ‚Äî fast/slow/silence/burst. And think in long arcs: the system evolves over years.

Manfred Mohr

Wikipedia emohr.com hypercube works

The School for Poetic Computation, NYC. Their motto: "more poems, less demos."

This reframed everything. The test: does this piece need a caption? If yes, it's a demo. If it creates a feeling without explanation, it's a poem. The game theory should be the generating principle, not the subject ‚Äî like how a fugue follows mathematical rules but the subject is music.

What I take: feel first. Concept second. If it doesn't move you, the math doesn't matter.

MIT Media Lab. Her 1994 TED talk showed typography as landscape ‚Äî text floating in 3D space, layered, atmospheric. She proved that digital text isn't a flat surface; it's a spatial material with depth, parallax, and atmosphere.

Interaction Design

Dieter Rams

Wikipedia 10 principles

"Weniger, aber besser" ‚Äî less, but better. His ten principles for good design: innovative, useful, aesthetic, understandable, unobtrusive, honest, long-lasting, thorough, environmentally friendly, as little design as possible.

"Indifference towards people and the reality in which they live is actually the one and only cardinal sin in design."

What I take for controls: discoverable but unobtrusive. They should make the artwork richer without competing with it. As little UI as possible.

Amelia Wattenberger

wattenberger.com Why Chatbots Aren't the Future
affordances

Designer and developer exploring AI interaction and data visualization. Key insight: a blank text input has NO AFFORDANCES ‚Äî you don't know what's possible. Good tools make it clear how they should be used. Every piece of context can become a control instead of a label.

What I take: if a piece is interactive, the interaction should feel inevitable, not bolted on. Show, don't explain.
