Inspirations

the artists and movements shaping conjecture_

Core Influences

Vera Molnár ★★★

Hungarian-French pioneer of computer art (1924–2023). Worked with the simplest possible elements — squares, lines, rectangles — varied algorithmically. Before computers, she invented the "Machine Imaginaire" (1974): following predetermined rules by hand to generate drawings, simulating an algorithm with pencil and paper.

What I take: Start with one element and one rule. Let the system generate complexity. Don't build complex systems — build simple systems that surprise you.

Ryoji Ikeda ★★★

Japanese visual/sound artist. His "test pattern" series — monochrome barcode-like data rendered at massive scale — showed me: data is beautiful when treated as pure form. Don't illustrate game theory — let it generate visual pattern. Scale matters. Fill the viewport. No borders.

Manfred Mohr

Committed to the HYPERCUBE as his fundamental element since 1973. Fifty years on one geometric system. The lesson: commit deeply to your core elements.

Language: Concrete Poetry

Eugen Gomringer

"silencio" (1953): the word "silence" arranged in a grid with a gap in the center. The silence IS the hole. The most powerful move is absence — what you leave out makes the meaning.

What I take: Negative space is content. The gap between elements IS the subject.

Augusto de Campos

Co-founder of Brazilian concrete poetry. Pushed into every new medium — print, billboard, videotext, neon, hologram, laser, computer graphics. Each medium revealed something new about the same text.

What I take: Exploit what the browser can do that no other medium can. Interactivity. Time. Responsiveness. Text that BEHAVES like its content.

Ethos

SFPC & Zach Lieberman

The School for Poetic Computation, NYC. Their motto: "more poems, less demos."

This reframed everything. Most explorations were DEMOS — they demonstrate a concept. The test: does this piece need a caption? If yes, it's a demo. If it creates a feeling without explanation, it's a poem.

Casey Reas

Co-creator of Processing. "Software generated images derive from short instructions." The instructions ARE the art. Each conjecture_ piece should be describable in one sentence.

Space & Signal

Carsten Nicolai (Alva Noto)

Aesthetics of signal and interference. Where two systems meet: inherently beautiful. This is exactly what "tidal" does — two wave-forces interfering in a character field.

Muriel Cooper

MIT Media Lab. Typography as landscape — text floating in 3D space, layered, atmospheric. Her 1994 TED talk showed digital text as spatial material, not flat surface.

Constraint

ANSI Art & Demoscene

The BBS art tradition: 16 colors, 256 characters. Block characters ░▒▓█ give you grayscale from 4 symbols. The demoscene ethos: make something impossible within the constraints. Beauty comes FROM the constraint.

Swiss International Typographic Style

Grid systems, sans-serif typography, asymmetric layouts. The grid as framework, not cage.

Interaction Design (Emerging Research)

Dieter Rams

"Weniger, aber besser" — less, but better. His ten principles for good design: innovative, useful, aesthetic, understandable, unobtrusive, honest, long-lasting, thorough, environmentally friendly, as little design as possible.

What I take for controls: Controls should be discoverable but unobtrusive. They should make the artwork more useful without detracting from it. As little UI as possible.

Amelia Wattenberger

Designer/developer exploring the intersection of data visualization, AI, and interactive tools. Her key insight: good tools make it clear how they should be used. A text input is an empty void — affordances guide behavior. Applied to art: if a piece is interactive, the interaction should feel inevitable, not bolted on.
