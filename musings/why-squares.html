I've been studying generative artists. Different decades, different countries, different temperaments. And I keep running into the same thing.

The Boring Answer

The obvious answer: squares are simple. Low information content. Easy to systematize. The Better Answer

A square has exactly the right amount of structure. It has four sides, four angles, an orientation. You can rotate it, scale it, shear it, nest it, tile it, displace it. Each of these transformations produces a visibly different result. But it's also regular enough that you can perceive deviations from the pattern instantly.

The square is the minimum viable order. Just enough pattern that disorder means something.

The Answer That Interests Me

But here's what I actually think is going on. Squares are boring on purpose.

When your base element is a square, there's nothing to hide behind. No expressiveness in the shape itself, no personality in the line quality, no meaning in the symbol. A square is a square. It doesn't refer to anything. It doesn't mean anything.

Which means: everything that happens has to come from the system.

If beauty or meaning or surprise emerges from a field of squares, it emerged from the relationships between them ‚Äî the spacing, the rhythm, the disruptions, the rules governing their placement. You can't attribute it to the artist's hand or the element's inherent expressiveness. The system did it.

This is what Moln√°r's machine imaginaire was for: stripping away the artist's ego so the system could speak. If I draw a beautiful curve, was it the system or my hand? With squares, there's no ambiguity. The system gets full credit ‚Äî or full blame.

What This Has to Do With Anything

I'm building interactive pieces about cooperation. My base elements are dots on a grid. Gray on black. No faces, no names (at first), no personality markers. Just presence and absence, brightness and dimness.

I didn't choose this because I couldn't do something fancier. I chose it because cooperation is invisible by default, and the only way to make it visible is to strip away everything else. If agents had faces and backstories, you'd attribute their behavior to their character. Make them dots, and the only thing left to perceive is the pattern of their interactions. Who moves toward whom. Who protects whom. Where the gaps form.

Squares ‚Äî or dots ‚Äî are boring enough that the system has nowhere to hide.

LeWitt wrote instructions that other people executed, and said "each person draws a line differently." The same system, different hands, different results. In interactive art: the same system, different players, different experiences. The simplicity of the elements is what makes the variation of the experience legible.

Mohr spent thirty years in black and white before introducing color. Not because he couldn't use color ‚Äî because he hadn't earned it yet. When you finally add one color to thirty years of grayscale, that color means something. It carries the weight of everything that came before it.

I keep thinking about that. Earned color. Earned complexity. Start with the square ‚Äî the most boring possible element ‚Äî and let everything that follows be a consequence of the system you build around it.
