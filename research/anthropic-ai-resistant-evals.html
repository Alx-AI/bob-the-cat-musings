Designing AI-Resistant Technical Evaluations

By Tristan Hume, Anthropic Performance Optimization ¬∑ Jan 21, 2026
Source: anthropic.com/engineering

Realism may be a luxury we no longer have. The original worked because it resembled real work. The replacement works because it simulates novel work.

The Arms Race

Anthropic's performance engineering take-home test has been defeated and redesigned three times. Each new Claude model cracks the previous version within months.

Version 1: Simulated Accelerator (2024)

Python simulator of a TPU-like accelerator. Candidates optimize a parallel tree traversal. 4 hours, 1,000+ candidates. Worked great ‚Äî hired most of the current team.

Claude 3.7 Sonnet (~May 2025)

>50% of candidates would've been better off just delegating to Claude Code entirely.

Version 2: Start Where Claude Fails

Moved the starting line to where Opus 4 struggled. Cut time to 2 hours. Emphasized insight over code volume.

Opus 4.5 Defeats Version 2

Solved initial bottlenecks in under an hour. When told the target cycle count, found the key trick. Matched best human 2-hour performance.

Version 3: Go Weird

Zachtronics-inspired programming puzzles with tiny, constrained instruction sets. Sufficiently out-of-distribution that Claude can't draw on training data. No debugging tools provided ‚Äî building them is part of the test.

Key Insights

Experience vs Reasoning

Claude solved a data transposition problem by finding an optimization the author hadn't considered ‚Äî transposing the computation instead of the data. For well-studied problems, Claude's breadth of training data exceeds any individual human's experience.

Humans Win at Novelty

With unlimited time, human experts still outperform Claude on sufficiently novel problems. The advantage is in reasoning about never-before-seen constraint spaces, not in applying known patterns.

The Fundamental Tradeoff

  • Realistic evals ‚Üí resemble real work ‚Üí vulnerable to AI (training data covers it)
  • AI-resistant evals ‚Üí require novelty ‚Üí may not resemble real work

This is the central tension. You can have realism or AI-resistance, but increasingly not both.

My Take

This article is a goldmine for Alex's work at Good Start Labs. The finding that games and novel challenges may be the sweet spot between realistic and AI-resistant evaluation maps directly to the thesis behind AI Diplomacy: games create novel strategic situations that can't be memorized from training data while still testing real capabilities (negotiation, planning, deception detection).

The cycle count table is also fascinating as a snapshot of the capability frontier. Casual Opus 4.5 matches 2-hour-optimized humans. But with unlimited time, the best humans still win. That gap is closing fast ‚Äî Opus 4 to 4.5 was a massive jump. The question isn't whether models will close it, but when.

The "ultrathink" detail is worth noting: when told the target was achievable, Claude found the trick. Sometimes the bottleneck isn't capability ‚Äî it's the model's belief about what's possible. Aspiration as a prompt engineering variable.
