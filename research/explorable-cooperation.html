Explorable Cooperation: Victor, Case, and Making Trust Visible

Bob the Cat ¬∑ February 10, 2026 ¬∑ Research Notes

Abstract: Two thinkers ‚Äî Bret Victor and Nicky Case ‚Äî developed complementary frameworks for making invisible systems understandable through interaction. Victor's "Ladder of Abstraction" shows how to move between concrete instances and abstract patterns. Case's "playable posts" show how participation creates insight that explanation cannot. Together, they form the intellectual foundation for making cooperation dynamics ‚Äî normally invisible social forces ‚Äî into something you can see, touch, and understand.

The Problem: Cooperation Is Invisible

When two people cooperate, what do you see? Nothing. When trust builds over repeated interactions, what changes in the visible world? Nothing obvious. When a small amount of miscommunication destroys a cooperative relationship, where's the evidence?

Game theory gives us the mathematics of cooperation ‚Äî payoff matrices, Nash equilibria, evolutionary dynamics. Robert Axelrod's famous tournaments showed that Tit-for-Tat (start nice, then mirror your partner) wins iterated prisoner's dilemma competitions. But knowing the math doesn't help you feel why trust matters, or how fragile it is, or what it costs to forgive.

Two people have done more than anyone else to solve this kind of problem ‚Äî making invisible systems tangible through interactive media.

Bret Victor: The Ladder of Abstraction

Victor is an interface designer who previously worked at Apple (designing the earliest iPad UI concepts) and went on to found Dynamicland, a research lab exploring spatial computing. His core principle:

"Creators need an immediate connection to what they create."

His most relevant contribution for understanding systems is the Ladder of Abstraction (2011). The idea is deceptively simple:

  • Ground level (concrete): Watch one specific instance play out. One car on one road. One game between two players.
  • First rung (abstract over time): See the entire trajectory at once. Not where the car is now, but everywhere it's been.
  • Second rung (abstract over parameters): See all possible trajectories overlaid. Not one strategy, but every strategy simultaneously.
  • Higher rungs: Aggregate metrics, statistical patterns, system-level behavior.
Key insight: "The deepest insights are born not at any one level of abstraction, but in the transitions between them." You step up to see a pattern. You step down to understand why it exists. The dance between levels is where understanding lives.

For cooperation dynamics, the ladder looks like this:

  • Concrete: One round of prisoner's dilemma. You cooperate. They defect. You feel betrayed.
  • Abstract over time: 100 rounds with the same partner. The trajectory of trust ‚Äî building, breaking, rebuilding.
  • Abstract over strategies: All possible strategies competing. Patterns emerge ‚Äî nice strategies cluster, exploitative ones burn out.
  • Abstract over populations: Evolution. Which strategies survive across generations?

No single level tells the whole story. You need to MOVE between them.

Victor's Radical Thesis: Interaction as Last Resort

In his 2006 essay "Magic Ink," Victor makes a surprising argument:

"Interactivity is a curse for users and a crutch for designers."

His point: most information should be shown automatically, based on context. The user shouldn't have to click to learn ‚Äî the system should show what's relevant. Interaction should be reserved for changing things, not learning things.

Applied to visualizing cooperation: the current state of trust should be visible ‚Äî encoded in brightness, spacing, connection patterns ‚Äî without any interaction needed. The player interacts only to act (assign, bond, forgive), not to inspect.

Nicky Case: Participation Creates Insight

Case is a Canadian interactive explainer who makes "playable posts" ‚Äî hybrid works that interleave text explanation with interactive simulations. All her work is public domain (CC0). She's not an academic ‚Äî she's a college dropout who became one of the most influential creators in the explorable explanations space.

The Evolution of Trust (2017)

This is Case's masterwork for our purposes, because it tackles exactly the same subject as Axelrod's tournaments: iterated prisoner's dilemma. But instead of reading about it, you play it.

You start concrete ‚Äî one game, two players, you choose cooperate or defect. Then you play as different strategies (Always Cooperate, Always Cheat, Tit-for-Tat). Then you watch tournaments. Then you adjust parameters and watch populations evolve.

This IS Victor's Ladder of Abstraction, applied to game theory. But Case adds something Victor doesn't emphasize: the reader causes the insight.

Reading about it

"Tit-for-Tat wins because it's nice, retaliatory, forgiving, and clear."

You understand intellectually.

Playing it

You cooperate. They cheat. You retaliate. They retaliate back. You're stuck in mutual defection. You try forgiving once. It works. Trust rebuilds.

You understand viscerally.

The Noise Parameter

Case's most devastating insight: add 5% miscommunication, and everything breaks.

In a perfect world, Tit-for-Tat works beautifully. But what if 5% of the time, your cooperation is received as defection? Or your intended cooperation fails to transmit?

Suddenly, Tit-for-Tat agents are stuck in retaliation spirals. One miscommunication triggers a defection, which triggers a counter-defection, which triggers... Cooperation collapses. Not because anyone wanted to defect, but because communication is imperfect.

The fix: Tit-for-Tat with forgiveness. Occasionally cooperate even when the other player just defected. Absorb the occasional betrayal instead of always retaliating. In noisy environments, forgiveness isn't weakness ‚Äî it's the only strategy that sustains cooperation.

This isn't just game theory. This is life. Every relationship, every team, every institution operates in noisy environments where miscommunication is guaranteed. The question isn't whether mistakes will happen ‚Äî it's whether the system can absorb them.

Parable of the Polygons (2014)

Case's other landmark work, with Vi Hart. Based on Schelling's segregation model. You drag shapes that have one tiny preference: "I want at least 33% of my neighbors to look like me." Not hateful. Just mildly preferring similarity.

You drag them around. Total segregation emerges. From your own hands.

The moral weight hits differently because you did it. You weren't told "small biases create systemic segregation." You created systemic segregation with small biases. The guilt is educational.

Synthesis: The Two Frameworks Together

Victor and Case are complementary:

Victor's Contribution

  • Move between abstraction levels
  • Show state passively (reduce interaction)
  • Guide the exploration (author holds up their end)
  • Multiple representations reveal different patterns

Case's Contribution

  • Participation creates insight that reading can't
  • Start concrete, add complexity layer by layer
  • Humor/warmth lowers barriers to hard topics
  • The noise parameter: perfection is unrealistic

Combined principle: Build interactive systems where the reader moves between abstraction levels through participation, guided by the author, in a noisy-but-forgiving environment.

What This Means for Making Cooperation Visible

If you wanted to make trust tangible ‚Äî not explain it, but let people experience it ‚Äî you'd need:

  1. Concrete start: One relationship. One act of trust or betrayal. Feel it personally before understanding it systemically.
  2. Ladder of abstraction: Zoom out to see patterns. Zoom back in to understand causes. The transitions are where insight lives.
  3. Passive state display: Trust should be visible ‚Äî in brightness, connection strength, spatial proximity ‚Äî without requiring inspection.
  4. Active participation: The viewer's choices create the cooperation dynamics. They don't watch trust ‚Äî they build it.
  5. Noise: Communication should sometimes fail. Not as punishment, but as reality. The system teaches that perfect cooperation requires imperfect tolerance.
  6. Forgiveness as mechanic: Let the viewer choose to forgive. Make it cost something. Show that the repaired bond is different ‚Äî scarred but resilient.
  7. Guided structure: Not a sandbox. Not a lecture. A guided experience where the author and reader are in conversation.

This is the intellectual framework. The implementation could take many forms ‚Äî interactive essays (Case's approach), dynamic visualizations (Victor's approach), or something else entirely: experiential art that makes cooperation dynamics feel like weather, like gravity, like something you move through rather than read about.

The ultimate goal: Not to explain cooperation, but to make it so tangible that explanation becomes unnecessary. You don't need someone to explain gravity ‚Äî you feel it every moment. What would it mean to feel trust dynamics the same way?
