The Imaginary Machine

Before Moln√°r ever touched a computer, she built one in her head. She called it the machine imaginaire ‚Äî the imaginary machine.

"Instead of starting from 'I am a genius and every work I make is a masterpiece,' I instead come up with a system with which I can check what changes were made and what that change provoked in me."

One Percent

By the end of her career, someone asked how much disorder was in her recent work. "I don't know what percentage," she laughed. But the principle never changed. It just deepened.

What This Has to Do With Trust

I've been building interactive pieces about cooperation. Agents that glow, form bonds, protect each other, betray. And here's what Moln√°r taught me: cooperation is the grid. Betrayal is the 1%.

Think about any community. Most interactions are neutral or positive ‚Äî people cooperating, doing their thing, maintaining the pattern. Invisible. Then someone defects. Breaks a promise. Acts against the group. That's the rotated square. Suddenly the whole field ‚Äî every prior cooperation ‚Äî becomes visible because of the disruption. You don't see trust until someone breaks it.

Nicky Case's Evolution of Trust teaches this with game theory. Axelrod proved it with mathematics. Moln√°r shows it with straight lines and nothing else.

The Disorder Principle, Applied

In First Light, the first void that appears is designed to hit. You can't protect everyone ‚Äî someone will be consumed. The void is the 1% that makes every prior cooperation legible. You didn't notice you cared about those dots until one disappeared.

The Curated Output

Modern generative art has a problem: infinite variation, zero selection. Ship the algorithm, let it generate forever, call every output equally valid. Moln√°r disagreed:

"There are two perspectives here. I debated this a lot with Pierre Barbaud, the composer."

Barbaud argued that the whole program output has inherent value. Moln√°r picked favorites. She looked at what her systems produced and chose the outputs that provoked something in her. The human judgment at the end isn't a bug ‚Äî it's the whole point.

For interactive work, this means: the player can explore a space of possibilities, but that space should be carefully bounded. Not "anything goes" but "these are the meaningful variations." Every possible state of a piece should be worth seeing. Design the constraints tight enough that the algorithm can't produce garbage.
